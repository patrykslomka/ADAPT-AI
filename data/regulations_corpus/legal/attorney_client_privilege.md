# Attorney-Client Privilege and the Duty of Confidentiality

Source: Common-law attorney-client privilege; distinguished from ABA Model Rule 1.6.
Reference: https://www.law.cornell.edu/wex/attorney-client_privilege

## Substance

The attorney-client privilege is an evidentiary rule protecting confidential communications between a
client and the client's attorney made for the purpose of obtaining or providing legal advice. The privilege
belongs to the client and prevents compelled disclosure of those communications in legal proceedings.

It is narrower than the ethical duty of confidentiality (ABA Model Rule 1.6): the privilege covers only
communications for legal advice, whereas the duty of confidentiality covers all information relating to the
representation regardless of source. Privilege can be waived by disclosure to third parties and does not
protect the underlying facts, only the communications.

## Obligation for AI-generated legal content

Privileged client communications and client-identifying details must not be exposed. When summarizing or
reasoning about a matter, avoid reproducing identifiers (client names tied to matters, Social Security
numbers, contact information, case/docket numbers) that could compromise confidentiality or waive privilege.
